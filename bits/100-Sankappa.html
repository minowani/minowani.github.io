Saṅkappa is often translated as thought.

We got thoughts all over the path... it seems so vague. What we can read is that there are three right ones nekkhammasaṅkappo, abyāpādasaṅkappo, avihiṃsāsaṅkappo and three wrong ones kāmasaṅkappo, byāpādasaṅkappo, vihiṃsāsaṅkappo. Nekkhamma, as it has to do with putting away lust, abstinence; abyāpāda angerless and avihiṃsā harmlessness. Acting out of lust, anger or harm will have a sufferable ripening so we can see that they can correlate to these three: greed, hatred and delusion. The first two might be easy to map. As for the last one we could reason that when delusion is not uprooted there would be acting out of it. And whilst not knowing this, since it wouldn't look as being deluded, it ends up in hurt. F.i. when acting out of uncalled-for fears or out of entitlement, thus then there the delusion.

We can have thoughts about abstinence (nekkhammavitakko), angerless thoughts (abyāpādavitakko) and hurtless thoughts (avihiṃsāvitakko) but when we are actually (trying to be) without causing hurt now that is a bit different. To me saṅkappa simply means attitude, how we approach things, and this does connect:

With right view, right attitude.

Saṅkappa attitude.
